# A Decision Tool, Not a Diagnosis: Community Strategy for a New Steel Plant

*How a phase-gated, source-graded research engagement turned a pollution-and-livelihoods problem into an MD-facing decision instrument — not another report that sits on a shelf.*

## The problem

A steel manufacturer had just commissioned a new plant near a community already living with two hard realities: seasonal agricultural-residue burning that fouls the regional airshed, and a large pool of women's labor sitting outside the formal economy. Leadership wanted a multi-decadal community and environment commitment — but explicitly rejected the default output of this kind of engagement: a glossy CSR diagnostic that states problems everyone already knows and prescribes nothing actionable. The brief was blunt — a diagnosis alone is googleable. Leadership needed something they could actually decide against: which levers to fund, in what sequence, at what cost, with the arithmetic shown rather than narrated.

## The constraint

Every load-bearing number in the eventual decision model had to be either sourced field data or explicitly left blank — never a plausible-sounding estimate. That discipline made the deliverable honest, but it also meant the deliverable alone couldn't answer "is this worth doing"; it could only state the one cheap test that would.

## The approach

The engagement ran in gated phases, each closing before the next opened:

- **Evidence substrate first.** Before any narrative was written, the team assembled a source base of 26 curated documents and datasets — government directives, satellite fire data, groundwater and labor-force surveys, peer-reviewed studies — each tagged by tier (primary document vs. secondary/stale vs. paywalled-abstract-only) and by known gaps, so every claim traced to a rated source rather than a model's assertion.
- **Multi-model drafting + adversarial cross-check.** Findings were drafted across multiple independent model runs, reconciled, then stress-tested by outside domain reads — an agricultural-economy journalist on the residue-burning question, a regional gender-and-labor academic on the women's-work question — to surface where the draft had understated a cost or mis-modeled an incentive.
- **Findings → forward, never blended.** Phase 1 stated only what was true on the ground and was signed off by leadership before any prescription was allowed. Leadership then named the two priorities to carry forward — residue burning without new pollution, and women's economic participation — and Phase 2 opened scoped to exactly those two.
- **From diagnosis to decision tool.** Phase 2 didn't stop at a strategy note. It was rendered as an interactive, access-gated tool leadership could navigate directly, built around a unit-economics model rather than prose recommendations.

The centerpiece was a two-equation unit-economics skeleton — one for the residue-to-value chain (delivered price minus farmer reservation price minus all-in logistics cost — does the margin clear?), one for a woman's work-seat (does the wage she requires to leave home-dairy work fall at or below what the activity's output can fund?). Paired with it was a Terms of Reference for a small (~4-week, low-cost) field audit whose entire job was to fill the deliberately blank cells, with a pre-agreed decision rule: a non-positive margin or an unaffordable wage is a hard no-go or reshape signal, not a negotiating point. Every soft, single-sourced, or secondary figure elsewhere in the note was flagged rather than smoothed over.

## Outcome

Phase 1's ground-truth findings were signed off by leadership as an accurate account of the local situation. Phase 2's decision package — priorities, mechanism, unit-economics model, and the one field audit needed to close it — was delivered as a working tool rather than a static report, the ask reduced to a single yes: fund the cheap audit before any capital commitment. The throughline was that every stage existed to de-risk the next one cheaply, so leadership never had to fund a guess.

## What I'd do differently

The decision rule (non-positive margin = no-go) is clean but binary. A version with a sensitivity band around the breakeven point would give leadership a better sense of how close a "yes" actually is, rather than only which side of zero it lands on.
